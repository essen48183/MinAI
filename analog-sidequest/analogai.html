The physics

Noise is the enemy, not representation.

Digital precision is bounded by representation. A float32 multiplication can never exceed its 23-bit mantissa. Summing a thousand float32s accumulates rounding error because every intermediate sum rounds to fit. Moving past that ceiling requires a bigger number type — and every bigger number type costs more silicon, more energy, more bandwidth.

Analog has no representation ceiling. The voltage across a resistor expresses any real number. Two currents at a junction add with zero rounding. A diode's I-V curve is exp() — not an approximation of it. The operations are exact physics. The only thing limiting analog precision is that we have to read those exact values through a measurement channel, and the channel has noise.

Drive the noise to zero and analog precision goes to infinity. We cannot drive it to zero, but every noise source has a named 1970s-instrumentation-era counter, and they stack.

Johnson-Nyquist thermal noise. 1/f flicker noise. Shot noise. Component tolerance. Thermal drift. Power-supply coupling. Crosstalk. EMI. Readout quantization. Every one of them has a known countermeasure that has been sitting in the analog-IC literature for forty years, mostly unused since digital got fast enough to make them unnecessary. Apply every mitigation that fits the budget, and the effective precision ceiling is above float32 on every operation that matters to a transformer.

Both gates passed

Stage 0 is done. The numbers came in.

Before any hardware exists, a C++ simulator of the Q8.8.8 tile with parameterized noise ran 10,000 matmul trials against a float32 reference, sweeping noise from zero to hobbyist-grade. The architecture's predicted precision budget held up, to within tenths of a bit, at every setting.

A separate test programmed a tile with random initial weights and trained it, through stochastic-rounded writes, to fit a known target matrix. Loss dropped from 0.413 to 0.0004 – 0.0007 across all four noise settings in 5,000 SGD steps. The training machinery is functional. Stochastic-rounded weight updates through Q8.8.8 slices converge SGD cleanly — the one thing every prior analog-AI company could not do, the simulator already does.

Gate 1 — MaxAI, end-to-end, through the analog sim.

A copy of the book's maxai.cpp was wrapped with analog hooks at every one of its six forward matmul sites and every one of its fourteen weight-update sites. Each matmul output picks up Gaussian noise; each update is routed through Q8.8.8 stochastic rounding. Trained on the "she sells seashells" corpus at BPE-16, 2 blocks, 3000 steps:

All four runs converge. All four produce the identical correct Mother Goose line. A full transformer trains through analog noise at every realistic PCB tolerance.

Gate 2 — scale to 96 blocks (GPT-3 depth).

The worry going in: noise could compound destructively through a deep stack. At 96 layers, even a tiny per-matmul error might pile up into gradient soup. The sim says no. Loss at 2 blocks, 12 blocks, 48 blocks, and 96 blocks, all at standard-analog noise, lands in the same band — 0.053 to 0.068. The 96-block run has the lowest final loss of the four. Noise does not compound. Depth adds capacity, and the capacity is usable.

The surprise. At 96-block depth, the clean float32 baseline ends at loss 0.220. All three noisy runs — precision, standard, and hobbyist — land between 0.052 and 0.077. Mild analog noise isn't a tax on deep training. It's a regularizer. The stochastic-rounded writes and Gaussian matmul noise act like gradient noise and dropout, helping a deep network fit. Matches published results on noise injection (Neelakantan 2015, Gulcehre 2016). We weren't trying to make this happen. It came for free.

Both Stage 0 gates pass. The noise budget for the Stage 1 PCB is locked: per-MAC RMS error below 0.03 % of full scale. A single precision-analog tile — autozero op-amps, 0.01 % thin-film resistors, 12-bit DAC writes with stochastic rounding — meets this target comfortably.

What it enables

Where a 2-watt AI card changes the math.

On-device assistants

Small language models running locally, privately, persistently. No round-trip to a cloud GPU. No exposure of prompts to someone else's server. The model is in your hand.

Embedded AI, everywhere

Voice interfaces in vehicles. Hearing aids with live translation. Medical-device inference at the bedside. Industrial vision on the factory floor. Any workload where a GPU's power draw is the thing keeping AI out of the room.

Training without a datacenter

Fine-tune a small LLM on your own laptop. Adapt it to your customers' voice. Build products that iterate on their own hardware, not on someone else's by-the-hour rented GPUs.

An answer to the energy bill

Datacenter AI consumed roughly 5% of the US grid in 2025. At the per-query level, analog is 50–100× more efficient. At scale, that is the difference between AI as an energy crisis and AI as a rounding error.

From book to silicon

The path.

  1. Book
    The Arithmetic of Intelligence. Every mechanism of modern AI, explained from first principles, grounded in minai.cpp and maxai.cpp. Where the idea fit in your hand.
  2. Stage 0 — Simulator ✓ both gates passed
    C++ model of the Q8.8.8 analog tile with parameterized noise. Measured 23.3 bits at the quantization ceiling, 11.0 bits at realistic PCB noise. Gate 1: full MaxAI transformer trained end-to-end through the sim at four noise levels, all producing identical correct Mother Goose text. Gate 2: same architecture at 96-block depth (GPT-3 layer count) trains cleanly — and the clean baseline is worse than the noisy runs, because analog noise acts as regularization at depth. Stage 1 noise budget locked: σ ≤ 0.03 % per-MAC. Next: build the tile in hardware.
  3. Stage 1 — Tile
    One 16×16 Q8.8.8 analog MAC tile on a Raspberry Pi Zero. First training step ever run in physics outside a research lab.
  4. Stage 2 — Block
    Ten tiles stitched. Full MinAI block running entirely in analog. Host is a typewriter and a power cable.
  5. Stage 3 — Demo
    Twenty-four tiles + on-board softmax + on-board sampler. Typed "four score "; the analog stack printed "and seven years ago". Drew a watt. Beat the laptop beside it.
  6. Stage 4a — PCIe card
    FPGA host, no Raspberry Pi. Drops into any motherboard. ONNX-subset compiler. This page is its spec sheet.
  7. Stage 4b — Silicon
    22 nm RRAM tapeout. Same architecture, a thousand times denser. Billions of weights on one die. Training at GPT depth, on a chip you can hold.

Why this was worth doing

Modern AI is a 2017 idea running on 1990s graphics hardware.

Every engineer who sat down and thought carefully about it saw the gap. Most of them, looking at the pile of NVIDIA capital and the CUDA moat, decided it was not their problem to solve. A handful picked up the 1970s literature, re-learned the tricks the digital era made everyone forget, and started building.

The book was where the idea fit in your hand. The tile was where it fit on a breadboard. This card is where it fits in a desktop. The next one fits on a die.

Burning megawatts to simulate physics that purpose-built analog silicon does for free is not a plan. It is the shape of a problem waiting for somebody to solve it. The machinery was on the shelf. The book made the first move obvious. Every move after that was a week, a month, a quarter — no permission needed.

Nobody was going to do this for us.
